\section{Problem 1: VAE Synthetic Data with Low-Data Stabilization}

\subsection{Objective \& Methodology}
To evaluate synthetic generation for low-data stabilization, a \textbf{Conditional Variational Autoencoder (CVAE)} was trained on a \textbf{restricted dataset of 350 real examples per digit}, initially \textbf{augmented 10 to 20 times} via rotations, shifts, small scaling, and light noise. Latent vectors $z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,I)$ were sampled to produce \textbf{50,000 synthetic images (5,000 per digit)}. A \textbf{LeNet-5 "Judge" model}, trained exclusively on the real data, evaluated these samples using its \textbf{maximum softmax output as a confidence score (CL)}. The synthetic data was partitioned into three sets: \textbf{Set A} (All samples), \textbf{Set B} (\textbf{High-confidence, $\text{CL} \geq 0.9$}), and \textbf{Set C} (\textbf{Mid-confidence, $0.6 \leq \text{CL} < 0.9$}).

\subsection{Results \& Conclusion}
A new LeNet-5, trained on the 350 real examples combined with the selected synthetic samples, was compared against \textbf{350-real and 1000-real baselines} to evaluate optimal selection strategies (high-confidence vs. mid-confidence/diverse, GANs, and simple augmentation). Results demonstrate that VAE-generated data \textbf{benefits significantly from high-confidence selection (Set B)}. High-confidence data ($\text{CL} \geq 0.9$) provides \textbf{clean, high-quality reinforcement}, preventing the model from learning \textbf{blurry VAE artifacts}. Consequently, the VAE-augmented dataset \textbf{successfully reduced the need for real data}, achieving an \textbf{accuracy of 97.75\%}, which \textbf{matched or outperformed the pure 1000-Real baseline (97.50\%)}. This confirms that \textbf{generative models can effectively synthesize missing data}.
