\section{Problem 3: Understanding the Impact of Attention Mechanisms}

\vspace{-0.5em} % Pulls section and subsection closer
\subsection{Objective}
This problem explores the impact of incorporating a Spatial Attention mechanism into Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). We compare the performance (accuracy and computational cost) of a standard CNN against an Attention-augmented CNN across two distinct tasks: simple image classification (Reduced MNIST) and complex audio feature extraction (Speech Spectrograms).

\vspace{-0.5em}
\subsection{Part A: Reduced MNIST Image Classification}

\textbf{Methodology:} 
We built a standard CNN (similar to LeNet-5) to classify handwritten digits. A second version of the CNN was created with a Spatial Attention mechanism (a $7\times7$ spatial mask) injected after the second convolutional block.

\subsection{Insights, Observations, and Future Improvements}
\begin{itemize}
    \setlength\itemsep{0em} % Removes space between bullet points
    \item \textbf{Observations:} The computational cost of Attention is consistent; calculating the spatial mask requires extra Conv2D and Pooling operations, invariably increasing the training time.
    \item \textbf{Hard vs. Soft Attention:} For datasets like MNIST, hard Attention mechanisms (cropping images based on bounding boxes) might be more effective than soft, continuous spatial masks.
    \item \textbf{Channel vs. Spatial Attention:} Experimenting with Channel Attention (e.g., SENet) could yield better results. For speech, it would learn \textit{which} mel frequency bands matter most, rather than just \textit{where} pixels are located.
\end{itemize}
